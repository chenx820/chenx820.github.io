My given name is Chen, which means "dawn" or "early morning" in Chinese. This meaning suits me well, as I am a morning person who enjoys the calm and peacefulness of the early hours. Interestingly, "Chen" sounds similar to "Cheng", the Chinese word for "orange". Because of this playful resemblance, my friends affectionately call me "Cheng-Zi", which translates to "orange".

My surname is Huang, which means "yellow" in Chinese. This name has deep historical and cultural roots. It is often associated with the Yellow Emperor, a legendary figure considered one of the founding ancestors of Chinese civilization.

Research Experience

  • Quantum Optics and Laser Science (QOLS) Group, Imperial College London
    Jun. 2024 - Present
    Under the guidance of Dr. John Michniewicz, I focus on investigating charge noise in semiconductor spin qubits for quantum computing. My work includes wire bonding quantum devices for characterization and studying the charge transport properties of semiconductor quantum dots at cryogenic temperatures using a dilution refrigerator. To enhance experimental efficiency, I developed an automated Python package to standardize communication protocols across experimental devices, significantly improving the measurement workflow.

  • Quantum Operating System Group, Beijing Academy of Quantum Information Sciences (BAQIS)
    May 2024 - Present
    As a remote intern at BAQIS, I focus on Rydberg atom quantum computing and compilation, a rapidly advancing area of quantum research. In this work, I introduce ZAP, a zoned architecture that separates the computational area into distinct storage and entanglement zones. Through ASAP scheduling and a simulated annealing algorithm, ZAP optimizes qubit placement and movement paths, fully harnessing the dynamic connectivity and large-scale parallelism of neutral atom platforms, and achieving a significant increase on fidelity. This work was published on arXiv.

  • Institute for Quantum Computing, Baidu, Inc.
    Mar. 2023 - Sep. 2023
    Working under Dr. Jingbo Wang, I developed an automated calibration framework for trapped-ion quantum computers, enabling precise determination of phonon frequencies and Lamb-Dicke parameters. This Python-based tool reduced manual intervention, improved parameter accuracy, and resulted in three patents. The rigorous industrial training at Baidu honed my coding skills, emphasizing code quality, documentation, and maintainability, ensuring future usability of the developed tools.

  • International Joint Lab on Quantum Sensing and Quantum Metrology, HUST
    Apr. 2019 - Dec. 2022
    For my graduation project, Nanoscale Detection of Ions Using a Spin Quantum Sensor, I focused on NV center quantum sensing. Combining theoretical calculations with simulations, I derived the relationship between spin coherence time and ion concentration. Additionally, I developed a 2D axisymmetric model for a Surface Forces Apparatus cavity and conducted finite element analysis using COMSOL, achieving strong agreement between analytical results and simulations.

  • Bradley Lab, University of Saskatchewan - Saskatoon
    Jun. 2021 - Oct. 2021
    As part of the MITACS Globalink Research Internship , I contributed to the project New Plasma Processing Techniques for Advanced Materials under the guidance of Prof. Michael Bradley. Due to COVID-19 travel restrictions, this work was conducted remotely, where I focused on coding tasks related to plasma motion while gaining insights into plasma processing techniques. Innovative Base of Physics Experiments (IBPE), HUST

During May 2019 - July 2020, I was the chairperson of IBPE. I was the only female leader of the platform since its establishment in 2002.

Feymann reading group

In collaboration with my team, I organized and led reading group activities to help first-year students adapt to university-level learning. We divided students into small groups of 4-5, with each group assigned a mentor from senior students. Weekly tasks centered around The Feynman Lectures on Physics were assigned, including theoretical derivations, experimental design, and simulations. Students collaborated within their groups to solve these tasks, while mentors offered guidance and suggestions. These activities not only deepened students' understanding of core physics concepts but also fostered a cooperative and engaging atmosphere within the department.

Chief editor of IRL (2020)

This is the inaugural issue of IBPE Review Letters (IRL), a journal my teammates and I established to preserve and share the intellectual output of IBPE members. While IBPE is known for its numerous reading and exploratory activities, many brilliant outcomes from past projects were unfortunately left undocumented, leading to the loss of valuable insights over time. To address this, we created IRL to publish key achievements from IBPE activities, contributions to the IBPE Annual Academic Meeting, and review articles or academic notes authored by IBPE members. We hope this initiative inspires new members and provides guidance for their research endeavors.
